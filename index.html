Good Visualizations

Joe Biden's Infrastructure Plan in One Chart


Marks:

This visualization uses area as a mark to encode each spending category.

Channels:

The chart uses color and size as visual channels. Color is used to differentiate between spending plans while size conveys the amount of money spent in each category and sub category.

Audience

The following visualization gives a detailed spending breakdown of Joe Biden’s Infrastructure Plan. Readers and subscribers of the New York Times are the audience of this visualization. However, more broadly, this visualization may reach voters who are interested in learning more about how Joe Biden’s infrastructure plan will be allocated. The audience may range from individuals who feel very passionate about expanding infrastructure spending to the less politically active individual who is curious about how his/her tax money might be spent and what benefits he/she might receive.

What data is encoded in the visualization?

Readers are able to view the spending breakdown of Joe Biden’s Infrastructure Plan.Viewers are able to differentiate between spending categories as well as spending differences between the Families Plan and the Infrastructure Plan.

When viewing this chart, readers are able to use color to view differences in spending plans and use size to compare spending amounts across categories.

What makes this visualization good?

This visualization is truthful in the sense that it encodes all the information contained in the infrastructure bill into a single graphic. It displays the size of spending with shapes that are proportional to the spending allocations in the bill. This visualization is functional because the chart accurately depicts the data in a way that is clear allowing people to make comparisons across different spending categories in the infrastructure bill. Looking at this chart, readers are able to identify the big picture and view each aspect of the legislation in the context of other spending categories. The simplicity of the design and color choices makes this chart beautiful, drawing the reader in with its visual appeal. The chart is insightful because it displays each component of the bill as a part of the whole so readers are able to understand what spending categories are being prioritized by the Biden Administration. The chart helps break down a highly politicized and potentially confusing bill into categories that are quickly and easily understood by readers. The clarity of this chart can enlighten readers, changing their minds on the spending bill and encouraging them to engage more with its passing.

Renewable Water Resources Per Capita (USA)


Marks:

The visualization uses the area mark to encode the amount of renewable water resources per year per capita in the U.S. from 1988 to 2014.

Channels:

The chart encodes three categories of water using the color channel. Each category of water is assigned a different shade of blue. The chart also uses the shape channel to mark 5 year increments along the chart. Additionally the chart uses poition channels. The x poition records the year (time) while the y position records renewable water per capita.

Audience

The audience of this visualization are those who are concerned about the water crisis and declining natural resources. More broadly, this visualization could appeal to those interested in environmental issues or worried about climate change.

What data is encoded in the visualization?

This visualization displays renewable water resources per capita in the United States over time.

When viewing this chart readers are able to recognize trends and see how renewable water resources are decreasing over time. Readers are able to compare availability of different types of water resources by looking at color encodings for different types of renewable water resources.

What makes this visualization good?

This visualization is truthful because it displays the information clearly and concisely using scales and axes that make sense considering the underlying data. The chart makes no attempt to hide or manipulate information. The chart is functional because it depicts the data in a way that is meaningful and easy to understand for the reader. Readers are able to easily make comparisons across categories and see change over time. Additionally, the interactive tooltip allowsthe readers to further interact with the data in a meaningful way. The color scheme and simplicity of the graph make it beautiful and aesthetically pleasing, encouraging the general audience to engage with the chart. It is insightful because it allows the readers to identify the severity of the water crisis by showing the decline in reusable water resources over 20 years. Readers will walk away from this chart enlightened and encouraged to think more about his/her water usage upon realizing that resources are decreasing.

The 30 Countries Where You Can Enjoy Ben & Jerry's


Marks:

This visualization uses points as marks to represent each individual flavor of ice cream.

Channels:

The chart uses color as a visual channel to encode ice cream flavor categories.

Audience

This visualization targets a general audience. In particular, it might target avid consumers of Ben and Jerry’s and ice cream lovers who are interested in learning more about what ice cream flavors are available around the world.

What data is encoded in the visualization?

The visualization displays the number of flavors of Ben and Jerry’s ice cream available around the world, breaking them down by flavor and type of ice cream (regular, light, and non-dairy). The visualization also includes a tooltip that provides more detailed information about each ice cream flavor and its availability.

When viewing this visualization, viewers are able to recognize flavor count across countries and ice cream categories. Additionally, viewers are able to recognize which flavors are most commonly sold in each country and compare flavor trends across countries and ice cream categories.

What makes this visualization good?

Above all, this visualization is functional. It breaks down the data in a way that allows viewers to easily recognize trends across categories of ice cream and countries where the ice cream is sold. Tooltips also contribute to functionality and decrease chart clutter by hiding less important details that the viewer can access by interacting with the chart. The clarity of the labels and simplicity of design make the chart truthful to the underlying data and no information is intentionally hidden from the reader. Additionally, the visualization is beautiful in the sense that it simplifies the data into an elegant and neat chart, encoding different categories with vibrant colors that contribute to the playful nature of the data and that draw the viewer in. The minimal design helps the viewer walk away from the visualization with an interpretation that is honest to the underlying data. Finally, this visualization is insightful and enlightening because it reveals flavor trends that readers would be unable to recognize when browsing ice cream in the grocery store. Although favorite ice cream flavor isn’t a pressing issue, readers can enjoy this fun chart and feel more connected to those around the world who share their favorite flavors.

Bad Visualizations

Confirmed Coronavirus Cases in Florida


Marks:

The rectangle mark is used to encode the number of covid cases. The length of each bar encodes the number of COVID-19 cases

Channels:

The chart uses color to differentiate between dates. Additionally the chart uses poition channels. The x poition records the date while the y position records the number of COVID-19 cases.

Audience

The intended audience for the above visualization is the Florida population, as well as the general public across the world who are interested in seeing how the number of COVID-19 cases has changed over time in different places.

What data is encoded in the visualization?

The visualization encodes the number of confirmed COVID-19 cases in Florida across a time period of five days.

When viewing this chart, individuals are intended to gather information about covid case rates in Florida and recognize case rate trends over time.

What makes this visualization bad?

This visualization is bad because it is untruthful. As we can see the height of the bars is not correlated with the actual values above them. The highest value, 4,049 is written above the bar with the smallest value. In addition, the visualization also only includes data across the timespan of five days. Adding a wider range of values that cover a larger time period would make the visualization more effective at displaying the change in Coronavirus cases in Florida. Adding more data points would add more context to the chart making it easier to see the impact COVID-19 has had on the population of Florida. This visualization also does not have a visible x or y axis. In this case one can assume that the numbers on the bottom correlate to a given date, but it is not explicitly stated. The absence of the x axis also made it easier to mislead the audience by not using a scale to make the difference between the different values visible. The visualization is misleading because it appears that the lowest number of cases occurred on 6/19, which actually has the highest value. In general, the lack of context and scaling leads the visualization to lack functionality and its misleading nature means the visualization lacks meaningful insight.

Looking Down on the Rest of the World


Marks:

This chart uses the cartoon figure to encode height.

Channels:

The chart uses poition channels. The x poition records the country while the y position records average male height.

Audience

The audience of this visualization is people who study demography, as well as people who are interested in learning and comparing population statistics across different countries. It is also intended for the general public as it has a playful and eye-catching design and displays a fun and approachable topic.

What data is encoded in the visualization?

The visualzation encodes the average male height in meters from several countries.

This visualization is a variation bar chart: it replaces the bars with cartoon figures to make the chart more visually intersting, attracting the audience's attention. When viewing this chart, individuals are intended to compare average male height from 6 different countries around the world. Individuals can identify which countries have the tallest average male height and shortest average male height.

What makes this visualization bad?

This visualization is bad because it is untruthful. When viewing this chart, individuals are led to belive that men from India and the Phillipines are significantly smaller than men from North America and Europe. The message is deceitful because in the graph the y-axis (heights) only range from 1.85 to 1.6. This limitted scale exagerates what would otherwise be more minor height differences by setting the y-axis baseline at 1.5m instead of 0m. Visually, this chart does not make sense because each figure is standing on the ground which assumes a baseline of 0. Therefore, not only is the chart confusing because it features figures standing on the ground (0m baseline) while the y-axis starts at 1.5m, but the chart also intentionally makes the Indian and Filipino figures much smaller and shorter, which misleads the audience.

Top 10 Movies by Worldwide Grosses


Marks:

This visualization uses area as a mark to encode money grossed by each movie.

Channels:

The chart uses size and color as visual channels. Different colors are used to represent the different movies, and the size corresponds to the total amount grossed by that movie. Additionally, the chart uses poition channels. The x poition records the year while the y position records movie production cost.

Audience

The intended audience for this visualization is the general public, but might appeal to those who are interested in film and the film industry and want to learn more about which movies grossed the most money.

What data is encoded in the visualization?

The visualization encodes the total amount grossed by specific movies against the total production budget.

The viewer is able to differentiate between the different amounts each movie grossed and compare that with the total production of each movie as well. The viewer is also able to observe the relationship between total amount grossed, and total production budget.

What makes this visualization bad?

This visualization is bad because the size of the bubbles is misleading. The difference between the values is not proportional to the difference in size between the bubbles. It also is somewhat difficult to see where on the circle lines up with the y axis, so the value of the production budget is not obvious to the viewer. One might assume that the center of the circle is where the value of the production budget is, but this is not explicitly stated to the viewer. Using bubbles to represent this data does not appear to be the most effective way to demonstrate the relationship between production budget and total gross for different movies. For these reasons, this visualization is not truthful because it is misleading and it is not functional because it is built in a way that makes it difficult for the viewer to make conclusions and compare values in a meaningful way.
